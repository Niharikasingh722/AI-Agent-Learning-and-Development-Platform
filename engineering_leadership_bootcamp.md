# Engineering Leadership Bootcamp — Course Reference

## Module 1: The Mindset Shift
Moving from Individual Contributor (IC) to Team Lead is the most significant career transition in engineering. Your output is no longer measured in lines of code — it's measured in team velocity, retention, and growth.

### Key Principles
- **Multiplier mindset**: Your job is to make each team member 10% more effective, not to be the best coder on the team.
- **Delegate to develop**: Give team members stretch assignments. Discomfort drives growth.
- **Psychological safety**: People perform best when they're not afraid to speak up or make mistakes.

---

## Module 2: Giving Effective Feedback
Feedback is the most important tool a leader has. Most leaders give too little, too late, or too vaguely.

### The SBI Framework (Situation–Behavior–Impact)
1. **Situation**: Describe the specific context ("In yesterday's architecture review...")
2. **Behavior**: Describe the observable behavior ("...you interrupted Alice twice before she finished explaining...")
3. **Impact**: Describe the effect ("...which caused her to disengage and the team missed her perspective.")

### Rules for Feedback
- Give feedback as close in time to the event as possible
- Focus on behavior, never on personality
- Positive feedback is as important as corrective feedback — balance matters
- Ask for a response: "What are your thoughts on that?"

## Module 3: Conducting 1:1 Meetings
A 1:1 is the most important recurring meeting you own. It belongs to the team member, not to you.

### Agenda Template
1. How are you doing? (5 min — personal check-in)
2. What's blocking you? (10 min — remove obstacles)
3. Career development (5 min — long-term growth)
4. Feedback for you as a manager (5 min — your own growth)

### Anti-patterns to Avoid
- Using 1:1s as status updates (use async tools instead)
- Canceling 1:1s when busy — signals that team members are low priority
- Doing all the talking

## Module 4: Code Review Best Practices
As a lead, your code review culture shapes the entire team's quality standards.

### What Good Reviews Cover
- Correctness: Does the code do what it claims?
- Readability: Can a new team member understand this in 6 months?
- Test coverage: Are edge cases covered?
- Security: Any injection risks, exposed secrets, or unvalidated inputs?
- Performance: Any obvious bottlenecks for the expected data scale?

### Tone Guidelines
- Prefer questions over commands: "What do you think about extracting this into a helper?" vs. "Extract this."
- Acknowledge good work explicitly
- Distinguish blocking issues from suggestions: prefix with `[blocking]` or `[nit]`
- Aim to complete reviews within one business day

## Module 5: Mentoring Junior Engineers
Mentoring is different from managing. It's a voluntary, trust-based relationship focused on long-term growth.

### The GROW Model
- **Goal**: What does the mentee want to achieve?
- **Reality**: Where are they now? What's getting in the way?
- **Options**: What could they do? (don't give answers — ask questions)
- **Will**: What will they commit to doing before next meeting?

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How do I handle a team member who consistently misses deadlines?**
First, understand why — is it capacity, clarity, motivation, or skill? Have a direct 1:1 conversation using SBI: describe the pattern, describe the impact on the team, and ask what support they need. Document the conversation. If the pattern continues, escalate to a formal performance improvement plan with HR.

**Q: How do I earn trust as a new team lead, especially if I was promoted from within the team?**
Be consistent: do what you say you'll do. Be transparent about decisions you can share, and honest when you can't share details. Don't pretend to have all the answers. Ask for your team's input on decisions that affect them. The transition is awkward — acknowledge it openly.

**Q: How do I balance coding vs. leadership duties?**
Most first-time leads try to do both at full capacity and burn out. The general rule: spend no more than 20-30% of your time coding in your first 6 months as a lead. Focus on unblocking your team, process improvements, and 1:1s. You will feel unproductive at first — that's normal.

**Q: How do I give feedback to someone who gets defensive?**
Stay calm and factual. Don't back down from accurate feedback, but do acknowledge their feelings: "I can see this is frustrating to hear." Separate the conversation if emotions escalate: "Let's pause and continue this tomorrow." If defensiveness is chronic, it's a coaching conversation about self-awareness.

**Q: What's the difference between coaching and mentoring?**
Coaching is task/goal-oriented and time-bounded (often paired with a specific project or development goal). Mentoring is relationship-oriented and long-term. A coach doesn't need domain expertise; a mentor usually does. You'll naturally do both as a team lead.
